## Data Governance Is Health Governance

Data is power. In healthcare, it’s also accountability, visibility, and trust. Yet in the Philippines, the governance of health data remains scattered — unclear, inconsistent, and reactive. The National Health Data Repository (NHDR), two years in, still doesn’t have a defined data governance model.

That vacuum isn’t just slowing implementation. It’s undermining the foundation of Universal Health Care (UHC). You can’t govern a health system you can’t see — and you can’t see what you can’t measure or trust.

Effective health governance **depends on data governance**. They are not separate disciplines; they are the same conversation viewed from different sides.

------

### The Governance Void

Right now, the Philippine health data environment operates without a single point of accountability.

- **DOH** issues policy but doesn’t own the data pipelines that support implementation.
- **PhilHealth** manages claims data but doesn’t align its data model with DOH systems.
- **DICT** sets infrastructure standards but not sector-specific governance rules.
- **NPC** enforces privacy law but doesn’t define health-sector-specific data-sharing frameworks.
- **LGUs** collect massive amounts of data but have no legal clarity on how they can share or analyze it.

This patchwork creates confusion and risk. Each actor protects its data for fear of violating privacy laws, losing control, or being blamed for breaches. The result: stagnation.

Without an explicit governance framework, everyone becomes cautious — and cautious systems don’t exchange data.

------

### The Myth of Ownership

One of the biggest misconceptions in health data governance is the idea that someone “owns” the data. That’s not how it works.

- **Patients** own their health information in principle.
- **Providers** are custodians — responsible for accuracy, privacy, and safe use.
- **DOH and PhilHealth** are stewards — responsible for using data for the public good.
- **NPC** is the regulator — ensuring privacy and consent compliance.

Governance is about balancing those roles through explicit rules, not about declaring ownership.
When everyone claims to own the data, no one can use it.

------

### Data Governance Defined

Data governance is not a policy manual. It’s a living framework that defines:

1. **Who can access what data.**
2. **Under what authority and conditions.**
3. **How it should be protected and shared.**
4. **How its quality and accuracy are maintained.**
5. **Who is accountable when things go wrong.**

For NHDR, that means formalizing how local systems, registries, and national agencies interact — from data entry to policy analytics — under clear, enforceable rules.

------

### Why It Matters for Health Governance

Good data governance creates three outcomes that mirror the goals of UHC itself:

1. **Accountability:**
You can’t hold systems accountable without trustworthy data. Health outcomes, spending efficiency, and equity all depend on verifiable, comparable data across levels.
2. **Transparency:**
Data governance ensures that information about performance, budgets, and outcomes isn’t hidden behind bureaucratic barriers. Transparency breeds trust — both among institutions and with the public.
3. **Responsiveness:**
Real-time, reliable data enables timely decisions — outbreak responses, resource allocation, workforce deployment. Governance ensures that those data flows remain lawful and ethical.

In other words, **data governance operationalizes health governance.** It turns policy intent into measurable reality.

------

### What Effective Health Data Governance Looks Like

A mature data governance structure is both **technical** and **institutional**.
It has systems, standards, and protocols — but also mandates, oversight, and accountability mechanisms.

Here’s what that could look like for the Philippines:

#### 1. National Health Data Governance Board (NHDGB)

A formal inter-agency body created under DOH, composed of DOH, PhilHealth, DICT, PSA, and NPC representatives — plus provincial and private-sector members.
Its mandate:

- Define national policies for data sharing, interoperability, and privacy in health.
- Approve and maintain the **NHDR Data Governance Framework**.
- Oversee the operation and compliance of the NHDR and registries.
- Resolve data disputes and clarify custodianship.

This Board should report directly to the Secretary of Health but operate with technical autonomy.

#### 2. Provincial and City Health Data Stewards

Each Province- and City-Wide Health System (P/CWHS) should appoint a **Data Steward** responsible for managing and securing local data exchanges, ensuring compliance with national standards, and coordinating with the NHDGB.

This ensures that governance is **distributed** — not centralized — mirroring the federated “network of networks” model of the NHDR.

#### 3. Defined Data Classifications

All health data should be categorized by sensitivity and use case:

- **Identifiable Data** (e.g., patient records) — shared only under consent or legal mandate.
- **Operational Data** (e.g., facility performance) — accessible to authorized agencies.
- **Aggregate/Public Data** (e.g., health indicators) — open for policy and research use.

This classification allows data to move safely without freezing the system in fear of privacy breaches.

#### 4. Common Data Access Protocol

Every system connecting to NHDR must use the same process for authentication, authorization, and audit logging.
This isn’t just a technical feature — it’s governance codified in software.

#### 5. Data Quality and Validation Framework

Governance isn’t just about who can access data — it’s about ensuring data is worth accessing.
Quality metrics (completeness, timeliness, accuracy, consistency) should be embedded in every NHDR integration and tied to compliance or incentive mechanisms.

------

### Bridging Privacy and Use

The **Data Privacy Act (DPA)** was never meant to stop data sharing; it was meant to make it safe and transparent. Unfortunately, fear of non-compliance has paralyzed many health offices.

Effective governance bridges privacy and utility by making **data sharing lawful by design** — through data-sharing agreements, anonymization standards, and clear consent models.

NPC should be part of NHDR’s governance not as an obstacle but as a partner — ensuring privacy is embedded, not bolted on.

------

### Governance as Culture, Not Just Structure

The hardest part of governance isn’t the policy — it’s the mindset shift.
Health officials must move from “owning” data to **stewarding** it.
Providers must see data submission not as compliance but as contribution.
Patients must trust that their data will be used for their benefit, not exploited.

That trust can’t be legislated; it’s earned through transparency, reliability, and accountability — the pillars of governance culture.

------

### Why NHDR Needs Governance Before Expansion

Without clear governance, every new feature of NHDR adds risk:

- Each new connection creates privacy exposure.
- Each registry synchronization risks duplication or inconsistency.
- Each analytic dashboard risks misuse if roles aren’t defined.

Governance ensures these innovations scale safely and sustainably. It’s what allows NHDR to evolve without collapsing under complexity or mistrust.

------

### How to Institutionalize It

1. **Legislate authority.**
The UHC Act and its IRR should be amended to explicitly define health data governance, recognizing data as a public good managed under stewardship principles.
2. **Fund it properly.**
Governance isn’t free. The NHDR needs a permanent secretariat and technical staff to manage compliance, monitoring, and audits.
3. **Integrate with DICT’s digital governance frameworks.**
Align NHDR data governance with the Philippine Data Interoperability Framework (PDIF) and the eGov PH architecture to ensure cross-sector consistency.
4. **Mandate annual transparency reports.**
Every agency connected to NHDR should publish an annual summary of data collected, shared, and used — proving that governance leads to accountability.

------

### The Bottom Line

Health data governance isn’t paperwork. It’s the invisible infrastructure that makes everything else possible — trust, accountability, innovation, and safety.

Without it, the NHDR will never move beyond pilot projects. With it, the Philippines can finally have a health data system that’s secure, interoperable, and ethically grounded.

Data governance **is** health governance. It’s how the country will know if its health system is working — and whether it’s working for everyone.
