## OpenHIE, Not Open Promises: The Framework NHDR Should Have Started With

The National Health Data Repository (NHDR) was supposed to be the data backbone of Universal Health Care (UHC). Instead, it’s become a recurring slide deck — technically ambitious but directionless, bureaucratically heavy but operationally empty. The intent was noble: to unify health data across the Philippines. The execution, however, stalled because it tried to design an outcome without a framework.

If the NHDR is ever going to be more than a talking point, it needs to start where every successful national health information exchange has started: with **OpenHIE** — the *Open Health Information Exchange* framework.

OpenHIE is not another software or system; it’s a **proven architectural approach** for building interoperable health systems in developing countries. It’s modular, standards-based, and designed for environments with limited resources — exactly like the Philippines.

------

### The Problem: NHDR’s Architectural Blind Spot

The NHDR, as originally designed, tried to be everything at once — a national warehouse, a reporting portal, a data exchange, and a governance mechanism. It defined outputs (“a repository”) without defining architecture (“how data should flow”).

That’s like trying to build a hospital without blueprints. You know you need rooms and wards, but not how plumbing, wiring, or oxygen lines will connect them.

The result? Paralysis. Agencies couldn’t align. Vendors couldn’t integrate. Hospitals couldn’t prepare. Everyone was waiting for standards and systems that never materialized.

------

### What OpenHIE Actually Is

OpenHIE (Open Health Information Exchange) was developed by global health organizations and governments to solve exactly this problem — how to create scalable, interoperable health systems in complex, resource-limited settings.

It isn’t a product; it’s a **reference architecture** composed of loosely coupled components that can be implemented independently and gradually connected.

The architecture is built on three core ideas:

1. **Foundational Registries:**
Authoritative sources for core health data — patient, provider, facility, terminology, and product registries.
These ensure everyone uses the same identifiers and codes.
2. **Interoperability Layer (Health Information Mediator):**
The “post office” that routes and translates data between systems (EMRs, LIS, HIS, claims).
It enforces standards (like HL7, FHIR, or even CSV schemas) and manages data sharing rules.
3. **Shared Health Record and Analytics Services:**
Systems that store, process, and analyze data for care continuity, policy, and planning.
These connect to the interoperability layer — not directly to every system — making scaling manageable.

This modular design means a country can build one component at a time, link them through APIs, and scale as capacity grows.

------

### Why OpenHIE Fits the Philippine Context

1. **It matches the devolved governance model.**
The Philippines operates under a decentralized health system where provinces and cities manage their own health networks. OpenHIE’s federated architecture naturally supports this. Each Province- or City-Wide Health System (P/CWHS) can have its own instance of core registries and shared health record that synchronizes with national systems.
2. **It’s designed for hybrid environments.**
OpenHIE doesn’t assume everyone is on the same standard. It can handle HL7 v2, FHIR, JSON, or even CSV.
That means hospitals can keep their existing EMRs while connecting through adapters — no need for forced replacement.
3. **It scales incrementally.**
You don’t need the entire country online to start. A single pilot province can run a full OpenHIE stack — registries, interoperability layer, shared record — and prove the model before national rollout.
4. **It’s open and vendor-neutral.**
No vendor lock-in. OpenHIE is built on open standards and open-source tools (like OpenHIM, HAPI FHIR, and OpenCR).
That aligns with public sector transparency, local innovation, and long-term sustainability.
5. **It’s already proven in similar environments.**
Countries like Rwanda, Tanzania, and Mozambique have successfully implemented OpenHIE-based national health information exchanges — despite facing the same resource and infrastructure constraints.

------

### How the NHDR Could Use OpenHIE

If DOH and PhilHealth align on OpenHIE, the NHDR can finally evolve from a static concept to a living ecosystem.
Here’s what that would look like:

1. **Build the Foundational Registries First.**

   - **Patient Registry (Master Patient Index)** – built around PhilHealth ID or a national health ID derived from PhilSys.
   - **Facility Registry** – standardized NHFR codes and metadata (type, ownership, region).
   - **Provider Registry** – PRC-licensed health professionals and staff mapped to facilities.
   - **Terminology Service** – a shared code system using ICD-10, LOINC, and SNOMED subsets.
   - **Product/Medicine Registry** – FDA-approved medicines and devices.

These registries should be accessible via APIs and synchronized between local and national levels.

2. **Deploy a Health Information Mediator (HIM).**
The HIM (e.g., OpenHIM) acts as the central router for all transactions — referrals, claims, lab results, case reports.
It validates messages, applies transformation rules, and logs transactions for audit.

3. **Implement Shared Health Records Regionally.**
Instead of one national database, each P/CWHS maintains its own shared health record that stores longitudinal patient data for local use.
These records can be aggregated at the national level for analytics.

4. **Connect National Programs and Analytics.**
The NHDR itself becomes a **federated data orchestration layer**, not a warehouse. It receives summary or de-identified data from regional systems for UHC monitoring, PhilHealth analytics, and disease surveillance.

5. **Institutionalize Governance and Standards.**
A national governance board defines how data is shared, who has access, and how consent is managed — supported by the NPC, DICT, and regional health offices.

------

### The Benefits of Starting with OpenHIE

- **Rapid Prototyping:** Start with one province and prove interoperability within months, not years.
- **Capacity Building:** Local implementers can learn and own each layer instead of waiting for top-down rollout.
- **Sustainability:** Modular components can be maintained independently; if one fails, the rest continue functioning.
- **Transparency:** Open-source tools ensure accountability and easier collaboration with academia and vendors.
- **Resilience:** Even if leadership or budget changes, the federated structure ensures continuity.

------

### The Alternative Is What We Already Have

Without an architectural framework like OpenHIE, NHDR remains a patchwork of disconnected projects — EMRs built in isolation, redundant data calls, and duplicated investments. Each agency builds its own system, hoping that “integration” will happen later.

But integration doesn’t happen later.
Integration must be designed from day one — at the architecture level.

OpenHIE provides that design. It forces clarity on what needs to exist (registries), how systems will communicate (interoperability layer), and where data will flow (shared health records).

The Philippines doesn’t need to reinvent anything. It just needs to implement what’s already been proven to work.

------

### A Practical Roadmap

If the DOH and PhilHealth adopt OpenHIE as the NHDR framework, here’s what a realistic roadmap could look like:

| Year            | Milestone                 | Description                                                  |
| --------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **2025**        | Architecture & Registries | Finalize NHDR canonical data model; build patient, facility, and provider registries; establish interoperability sandbox. |
| **2026**        | Pilot Implementation      | Deploy full OpenHIE stack in two pilot provinces; connect PhilHealth claims and Konsulta data. |
| **2027–2028**   | Scale-Up                  | Expand to 10 P/CWHS; national governance board operational; analytics dashboards live. |
| **2029 onward** | Institutionalization      | Integrate registries into core DOH and PhilHealth systems; shift from project mode to sustained operation. |

This approach builds progressively, delivers measurable results each year, and aligns with both UHC implementation and digital transformation timelines.

------

### A Call for Pragmatism

OpenHIE doesn’t sound glamorous. It’s not “AI,” it’s not “big data,” and it won’t win awards for innovation.
But it’s the backbone that makes everything else possible.

While the NHDR has spent two years lost in conceptual noise, OpenHIE countries have been exchanging real clinical data for nearly a decade — securely, at scale, and with tangible impact on patient outcomes.

It’s time to stop reinventing frameworks and start implementing one that already works.
The Philippines doesn’t need another promise of interoperability. It needs **OpenHIE — not open promises.**
